The Fictional Black Hole
Gargantua is the colossal black hole at the center of Christopher Nolan's 2014 film Interstellar. It orbits a star in a distant galaxy and is accompanied by the planet Miller, which sits so close to its event horizon that time dilation makes one hour on its surface equal to seven years on Earth.
The visual effects were created by the Double Negative team in close collaboration with physicist Kip Thorne, who wrote the scientific equations that drove the rendering software DNGR. The result was the most physically accurate depiction of a black hole ever shown in cinema.
Gargantua has a mass approximately 100 million times that of our Sun, a spin parameter of a* ≈ 0.998 (near maximum Kerr spin), and an accretion disk inclined for dramatic cinematic effect.
Real-World Physics
A Kerr black hole is described by the Kerr metric — a solution to Einstein's field equations for a rotating mass. Unlike a Schwarzschild black hole, it possesses an ergosphere: a region outside the event horizon where spacetime itself is dragged in the direction of rotation.
r+ = M + √(M² − a²)
a = spin parameter (0 ≤ a ≤ M)
The innermost stable circular orbit (ISCO) shrinks dramatically with spin: for a* = 0.998 it reaches r_ISCO ≈ 1.24M, compared to 6M for a non-rotating black hole. This allows the accretion disk to extend far closer to the horizon, extracting enormous gravitational energy.
T_Hawking = ℏc³ / (8πGMk_B)
T_disk ∝ (M/r)^(3/4) [Novikov-Thorne]

This Simulation
The rendering uses a WebGL2 ray-marching approach that numerically integrates null geodesics in the Kerr metric. Each screen pixel casts a photon ray that is deflected by the curved spacetime. When the ray passes through the accretion disk plane, a Novikov-Thorne temperature profile and fractional Brownian motion (fBm) noise generate the turbulent plasma structure. Relativistic Doppler boosting (g⁴ beaming), gravitational redshift (√g_tt), and frame-dragging (Lense-Thirring) corrections are applied per sample. The final color is tone-mapped with the ACES filmic curve and film grain is added for cinematic texture.
